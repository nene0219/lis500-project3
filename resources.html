Resources on Implicit Bias and Intersectionality

Implicit bias refers to unconscious attitudes or stereotypes that influence our decisions and interactions, often without awareness. The Implicit Bias Test (IBT) helps uncover these hidden biases, encouraging self-reflection. Intersectionality, coined by Kimberlé Crenshaw, highlights how overlapping identities—such as race, gender, and class—shape unique experiences of privilege and discrimination. Explore the resources below to deepen your understanding.

What is the Harvard IAT?

The Harvard IAT measures the strength of associations between concepts and stereotypes, such as linking “women” with “family” versus “men” with “career.”

Discussion

The creators use “automatic preference” to describe results, avoiding definitive labels of bias or racism. A Vox article noted the lack of disclaimers about its predictive limits, though recent updates clarify that a single test doesn't forecast behavior. While its effectiveness is debated, the IAT prompts valuable introspection on hidden biases.

In-Class Readings

Discussion Guide for "White Fragility" by Robin DiAngelo

Read Guide

This guide offers instructions for leading discussions on white fragility with white groups, addressing resistance and hopelessness. DiAngelo emphasizes: “The primary goal for white people working to understand racism is not to learn how racism impacts people of color. The primary goal is to recognize how the system of racism shapes our lives, how we uphold that system, and how we might interrupt it.”

Ben Shapiro's Take on Intersectionality

Read Article

Shapiro's critique presents a pessimistic view of intersectionality, illustrating how the concept can be misconstrued and negatively portrayed.
